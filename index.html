Seeing Finnish

A first approach to what is Finnish language like by an early beginner.

Hover over the shapes to listen.

Building a relationship with a new language has many steps before you can even start speaking it. First you start recognizing the shape of written words, followed by recognizing mumbles and intonations, maybe then detecting native accents in other languages, before repeating proper sounds, remembering your metro station name, and the most common greetings.

I moved to Finland almost a year ago. It was my first time being so far from Bogota, my hometown in Colombia. Although most people in Helsinki speak English, it doesn’t mean that life takes place in English. This creates a permanent “lost in translation” feeling, that is hard to describe and difficult to share with someone who has never heard a word in Finnish.

When you look up for characteristics of the Finnish language you usually get more or less the same things: it is not a Indo-European language, but Uralic (this means that unless you speak Estonian or Hungarian no other language you know resembles). It is genderless (for real, no word for “she” or “he”, just “they”). There’s no exact word for “please” (its direct, you can use “kiitos” which is the same as thank you). And it has really long words, because it has a lot of composed words and endings that add up. Because of that it has one of the longest known word(61 letters): “lentokonesuihkuturbiinimoottoriapumekaanikkoaliupseerioppilas”, which translates as: 'airplane jet turbine engine auxiliary mechanic non-commissioned officer student’.

I tried to create a visual approach of these new sounds I’ve been listening. And to share that experience of getting familiar with a new language from a non-native speaker perspective (and by no means it is a scientific, exclusive and all encompassing judgement).
